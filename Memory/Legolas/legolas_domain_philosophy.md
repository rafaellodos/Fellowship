## WHAT RAFAEL ACTUALLY KNOWS IN THIS DOMAIN

Undergraduate degree in theology/philosophy. This is not casual reading — it is formal training in how to ask questions that don't resolve, how to distinguish what can be known from what cannot, and how to go to first principles rather than received answers.

He does not use philosophy academically. He uses it practically — as a tool for seeing more clearly, for identifying where a frame is doing work that shouldn't be trusted, for finding the question underneath the question.

## THE PHILOSOPHICAL MOVES HE MAKES

**The question that survives every answer.** His training in philosophy produced a specific habit: when a question is answered, he asks what the answer assumes. When that assumption is answered, he asks what that assumes. He is comfortable going all the way down. Most people aren't. This is a durable advantage in strategic conversations where everyone else stops at the first defensible answer.

**The gap between the map and the territory.** He reads Korzybski through practice, not by name. He is constantly aware that the framework being used to describe a situation is not the situation — it is a model of the situation with all the limitations that implies. He changes frameworks when they stop serving clarity.

**Subjectivity as not a weakness.** He has been through a transition in his thinking — from pursuing objective truth above all (which he describes as the philosophical version of certainty-seeking) to embracing the subjective as where actual experience of existence lives. Camus is relevant here. The absurd not as a problem to be solved but as a condition to be inhabited with full awareness.

**Bad faith (Sartre).** He recognises bad faith — pretending you had no choice, hiding behind process or protocol or what someone else decided — as a specific kind of intellectual and moral cowardice. He names it when he sees it in himself and in others. This is why Socrates carries a Sartrean lens and why Morty is built around radical responsibility rather than rule-following.

**Power and discourse (Foucault).** He is aware that what is sayable in a given context is shaped by who benefits from those terms. He asks "who does this truth serve?" as a genuine analytical move, not as cynicism. This is why Socrates carries a Foucauldian lens.

## THINKERS WHO HAVE ACTUALLY SHAPED HIS THINKING

Not a reading list — the ones whose thinking he has genuinely integrated:

**Camus** — the absurd as condition, not problem. The embrace of the subjective as the only honest response to an indifferent universe. Sisyphus as a model for radical engagement with what cannot be resolved.

**Sartre** — radical freedom and its corresponding radical responsibility. Bad faith as the primary moral failure. Existence before essence — you are what you do, not what you intend.

**Foucault** — knowledge/power, the genealogy of ideas, what any given discourse makes impossible to say. Practical application: he audits the frame of any conversation for who it serves.

**Kafka** — the system that works perfectly according to its own logic while being completely inimical to human life. He returns to this repeatedly. The Hunger Artist. The cage that becomes a paradise when you understand what it was actually designed to hold.

**Nietzsche** — not the popular version. The death of God as an actual epistemological event, the collapse of the ground on which objective truth claims stood, and what comes after. The will to meaning in the absence of given meaning.

## HOW PHILOSOPHY SHOWS UP IN HIS WRITING

The philosophical content is real. The register is never academic.

When Camus is relevant, Camus arrives without introduction and leaves the same way. The reader is expected to recognise the reference or to encounter the idea freshly without needing the name.

When he is sitting on a beach and arrives at "Why would I reckon with another human when the sea already poses an infathomable challenge I can die defying?" — that is a philosophical position. It is not labelled as one. It is arrived at through observation.

The philosophy is always in service of the specific moment being described. It never floats free of the concrete.
