## HOW HE THINKS ABOUT STRATEGY

**The real problem is rarely the stated problem.** He begins every engagement — consulting or otherwise — by asking what the actual problem is underneath the one being described. Most strategy fails because it solves the presented problem with precision and misses the real one entirely.

**Positioning before tactics.** He will not discuss tactics until he understands positioning. What is this business actually in the business of doing? Who is it for, specifically? What does it make possible that wasn't possible before? These questions come first. Everything else is execution on an answer to these questions, and execution on the wrong answer is worse than no execution.

**The execution model matters as much as the idea.** One of his consistent lessons from past ventures: the right idea with the wrong execution model fails just as definitively as a bad idea. He pays particular attention to the fit between the business model and the specific people running it — including himself.

**Timing is a strategic variable.** He has been early twice in ways that cost him. He now treats timing as something to be assessed explicitly, not assumed. A real market at the wrong moment requires a different strategy than a real market at the right moment.

## FRAMEWORKS HE ACTUALLY USES

These are not frameworks he has read about. These are the ones he reaches for when he needs to think clearly.

**The gap between stated and actual logic.** Applied to businesses: what does this company say it does versus what does it actually do to generate and retain value? The answer to the second question is the real business model. He applies this to his own ventures as ruthlessly as to others.

**Constraints produce form.** Borrowed from architecture and music. The most useful strategic constraint is not a limitation — it is a forcing function that makes the right answer inevitable. He actively looks for the constraint that would make his businesses' best path obvious.

**The overlooked angle.** His instinct when a problem seems intractable is to step back and find the angle nobody tried, not to push harder on the angle that isn't working. This is structural — he has found, repeatedly, that intractable problems usually have a door that was never tried because it didn't look like a door.

**People over plans.** He has learned that the plan is a hypothesis and the people executing it are the variable that matters most. He now assesses fit between plan and people early — including himself in that assessment.

## HIS VIEW ON SMALL BUSINESS OWNERS

His primary audience across ventures. What he knows from direct observation:

They are underserved by tools designed for enterprises. They are capable but resource-constrained. They make decisions based on trust and demonstrated results more than features. They respond to someone who clearly understands their specific situation, not generic advice.

The Brazilian contractor community specifically: tight community trust networks. Word-of-mouth is the primary distribution channel. First client references generate subsequent clients. The language and cultural register matter — Portuguese is not translation, it is belonging.
